You've learned maneuvers in freestyle; now let's look at the arena where we direct your energy at a goal: **racing.** In drone racing, pilots try to complete a course in the shortest time by passing through a series of **gates** and flags. The secret here lies not in motor power but in the **line** you follow.

The <KeyTerm en="racing line">racing line</KeyTerm> is the ideal path you follow around the course. The one-sentence summary of this lesson is:

<Callout type="info">
In racing the goal is not to reach the **highest instantaneous speed**, but to finish the course from start to end in the shortest time with the **smoothest** (most flowing) line. This idea, well known from car and motorcycle racing, applies exactly the same in drones.
</Callout>

## Why "smoothest" and not "fastest"?

Instinctively you might think "the shortest straight line between gates is the fastest." But the reality is different: on a sharp, angular line you do a **hard brake + hard turn + re-acceleration** at every gate. Every hard brake kills your momentum (the speed you've built up), and regaining it takes time.

On a smooth line, on the other hand:

- You enter turns **wide and round**, so you keep most of your speed.
- As you exit a gate, you're already **aligned toward** the next gate.
- You **carry** your momentum instead of spending it; this shortens your total time.

## Gate entry and exit angle

A racing gate isn't just "a hole to pass through"; how you **enter** and how you **exit** matters. Because your exit angle from a gate determines your entry line into the next gate.

- **Entry:** Approach the gate at an angle that makes it easier to head toward the next target. Sometimes entering the gate slightly "from the outside" and exiting from the inside helps you align with the next gate.
- **Exit:** Ideally, as you exit a gate your nose is already pointing at the next gate. A good pilot, while passing through one gate, has their mind already on **the next two gates.**

<Callout type="tip">
A practical tip: focus not on individual gates but on the **transitions between gates.** Instead of "how do I get through this gate," think "how do I exit this gate and connect to the next most smoothly." Reading ahead (look ahead) is the most important mental skill in racing.
</Callout>

## Throttle management

The most common mistake of new racers is **constant full throttle.** Flying with the throttle pinned both makes control harder and throws the drone off the line in turns. The correct logic:

- On **straights**, open the throttle, accelerate.
- **Before turns and gates**, ease the throttle off smoothly; settle the drone onto the line.
- **On the exit of a gate**, open it up smoothly again.

So the throttle isn't an on/off switch but a tap you constantly and gradually adjust. In racing this is called "modulating the throttle."

## Consistency comes before speed

In racing, putting down one very fast lap and then missing a gate or crashing on the next lap is useless. What truly wins is **consistency**: being able to repeat the same smooth line lap after lap, flawlessly.

<Callout type="warning">
A pilot who pushes past their limit "to fly faster" and crashes every lap finishes **slower** than a pilot who flies a bit slower but clean; because every crash means recovery time (or even being knocked out of the race). Fly **clean and consistent** first; speed naturally builds on top of consistency.
</Callout>

The path to improvement is clear:

1. Be able to fly the line **clean** (flawless, even if slow).
2. Be able to fly the same line **over and over** the same way (consistency).
3. Only after that, **gradually speed up** while keeping the line.

<Callout type="info">
**Summary:** Racing line = the **smoothest** line, not the shortest; because carrying momentum is faster than braking and re-accelerating. Your gate entry/exit angle determines your alignment to the next gate; read ahead. Manage the throttle gradually like a tap. And above all, be **consistent** — speed comes on top of clean flying. </Callout>
