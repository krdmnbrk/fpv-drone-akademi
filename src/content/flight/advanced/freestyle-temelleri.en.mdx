## Transitioning from Angle to Acro

Before diving into freestyle maneuvers, let's build an honest bridge: at the beginning of these lessons you mostly flew in **Angle mode** (the drone levels itself out when you release the stick). Freestyle, however, is done in **Acro mode** — in Acro the drone won't save you; you close out the lean yourself. The transition from Angle to Acro is FPV's biggest threshold, and you need to cross it in **small steps.**

We recommend climbing these steps in order:

- **Orientation practice:** The hardest thing in Acro is sensing which way the drone is currently leaning. First, settle it in your mind that even when the drone's nose is **facing toward you**, the controls don't reverse (left-right doesn't swap). Losing orientation is the number one reason beginners crash in Acro.
- **First Acro hover in the simulator:** Without touching a real drone, try **holding steady in the air** in Acro mode in the simulator. You'll immediately feel the difference from Angle: the drone now wants a tiny correction at every moment and won't level out when you release the stick. Stay here until you can do this hover comfortably in the simulator.
- **Nose-in hover:** Once the Acro hover is settled, practice hovering with the drone's **nose turned toward you.** Since the controls will feel reversed like a "mirror," this truly tests your orientation skill. If you can do a nose-in hover, your orientation is solid.
- **Progress in small steps:** Acro hover → forward flight → gentle turns → small dives... Do each step dozens of times in the simulator first, then move to the next. Don't rush; a solid Acro foundation is the basis for all the maneuvers below.

<Callout type="tip">
Don't rush and jump straight from Angle to freestyle maneuvers. If you can't comfortably do an Acro **hover** and **nose-in hover**, it's too early to try a power loop or split-S. First settle orientation and the Acro hover in the simulator; without that foundation, the maneuvers end in a crash.
</Callout>

Once you can comfortably fly in Acro mode, do coordinated turns, and know your rates, the next step is **freestyle.** <KeyTerm en="freestyle">Freestyle</KeyTerm> is drawing acrobatic maneuvers with the drone — turning flight into a kind of "dance in the air." In this lesson we'll introduce the **first three basic maneuvers** of freestyle: the **power loop, dive, and split-S.**

<Callout type="info">
What these three maneuvers have in common is this: they all work with **momentum**, that is, the drone's speed and altitude energy. More than motor power, learning to channel energy to the right place is the real key to freestyle.
</Callout>

## First the concept: momentum management

<KeyTerm en="momentum">Momentum</KeyTerm> is the motion energy the drone possesses. It comes from two sources:

- **Speed** (forward/horizontal energy) — the energy built up when you apply throttle and fly forward.
- **Altitude** (potential energy) — the energy of being up high, which can be converted into speed in a dive.

In freestyle you don't perform maneuvers with "muscle power"; you do them by **channeling this energy from one form to another.** For example, in a dive you convert altitude into speed, then in a climb you convert that speed back into altitude. A good freestyle pilot isn't one who constantly punches the throttle, but one who **manages energy.**

## Maneuver 1 — Dive: learn this first

A <KeyTerm en="dive">dive</KeyTerm> is the most basic and most-used maneuver: tilting the drone's nose down and descending in a controlled way, then recovering and leveling at the bottom.

Why do we learn this first? Because the dive teaches you two fundamental freestyle skills:

1. **Throttle management** — In a dive you learn to cut throttle and gently apply it at the bottom. No punching the throttle in a dive; gravity already accelerates you.
2. **Recovery** — Turning the nose up and leveling off as you approach the ground. You'll use this reflex in every maneuver.

<Callout type="warning">
The most common and most dangerous mistake in a dive is **seeing the bottom too late**: underestimating how far you'll fall and starting your recovery too late. Always recover **early and smoothly.** Do your first attempts from high up with plenty of room to spare; dives close to the ground come with experience.
</Callout>

## Maneuver 2 — Power loop: built on top of the dive

A <KeyTerm en="power loop">power loop</KeyTerm> is rotating the drone a full turn in a vertical plane: you go forward and climb, come over the top inverted, and draw a loop backward to dive back toward where you started. Imagine looping over an obstacle (a bridge, a branch, a gate).

We learn the power loop **after** the dive, because the descending half of the loop is essentially a dive. If you don't know the dive and recovery, you can't control the exit of the loop either. The logic:

- **Entry:** Build up speed horizontally (momentum), then turn the nose up.
- **Top:** Roll over the top point while maintaining throttle; the drone goes inverted here.
- **Exit:** On the descending part, cut throttle (this is now a dive) and recover at the bottom.

## Maneuver 3 — Split-S: the acrobatic way to change direction

A <KeyTerm en="split-S">split-S</KeyTerm> is a maneuver used to quickly **reverse direction 180 degrees and descend**: you roll the drone inverted (half roll), then pull the nose down and dive into the opposite direction with a half loop. You end up flying in exactly the opposite direction, at a lower altitude.

We put the split-S last because it **combines** two skills: first a roll/flip, then a controlled dive and recovery. So it contains both the dive and the descent logic of the power loop.

<Callout type="tip">
The split-S is an altitude-"spending" maneuver: you descend a bit each time. So **start high enough.** Attempting a split-S from low is one of the most common ways to hit the ground with no room left to recover.
</Callout>

## A safe learning order

This ordering is no coincidence; each maneuver uses the skill of the previous one:

| Order | Maneuver | Why this order? |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Dive** | Teaches throttle management + the recovery reflex (foundation) |
| 2 | **Power loop** | Its descending half is a dive; can't be done without knowing the dive |
| 3 | **Split-S** | Combines roll + dive + recovery |

<Callout type="info">
General rule: **simulator first.** Before attempting each new maneuver on a real drone, do it dozens of times in the simulator. In the simulator you crash for free; crashing in the field breaks the drone and can even be dangerous.
</Callout>

## Choosing a safe and large area

Freestyle demands far more **vertical and horizontal space** than top-down flying, because you dive, climb, invert, and recover. When choosing an area:

- **Plenty of space:** In every direction — especially up and down — have more than enough room to recover. Try new maneuvers from high up.
- **No people:** There should be no people, animals, vehicles, or crowds around. There's always a chance of losing control during acrobatics.
- **Know the obstacles:** Identify the locations of obstacles like poles, wires, and trees by eye before flying.
- **Spotter and rules:** Since FPV doesn't count as line-of-sight flying, have a **spotter** beside you; in Türkiye, follow SHGM rules and no-fly zones.

<Callout type="warning">
Never try freestyle maneuvers near people, roads, or structures. Loss of control, a link drop (failsafe), or a bad recovery can happen at any moment; so **space is your safety cushion.** When in doubt, fly higher and in a more open area.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
**Summary:** Freestyle is managing momentum (speed + altitude energy). The safe order: first the **dive** (throttle + recovery), then the **power loop** (its descent is a dive), and last the **split-S** (a roll + dive combination). Learn each maneuver in the simulator first, and fly in a large, people-free area with a spotter. Now a mini quiz!
</Callout>
