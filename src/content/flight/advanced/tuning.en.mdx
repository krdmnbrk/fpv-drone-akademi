You've built your own drone and you know the PID/rate concepts. <KeyTerm en="tuning">Tuning</KeyTerm> is bringing the drone's flight behavior to the feel you want, through fine-tuning in software (Betaflight). But let's start with the most important message:

<Callout type="tip">
The **default (stock) settings** of modern flight firmware **are already very good for most drones.** Tuning is not "a mandatory step on every drone," but is for **solving a problem** or perfecting an already good flight. If you're just starting out: fly with the defaults first, and if there's no problem, don't touch it.
</Callout>

## When is tuning needed?

You turn to tuning not blindly, but when you see a **symptom.** Typical signs:

- **Vibration / oscillation:** The drone shakes in the air, "buzzes" while accelerating or under throttle, the motors overheat.
- **Blurry / jello-like video:** A vibration-induced wobble in the image (jello) — usually a filter/vibration issue.
- **Unresponsive or overly responsive feel:** The drone responds late to commands, or it's too twitchy/jumpy.
- **Hardware change:** If you switched to a very different motor/propeller/frame, the default tune may no longer be ideal.

<Callout type="warning">
Before starting to tune, **address mechanical problems.** A bent/cracked propeller, a loose motor screw, a poorly balanced part, or a loose frame is the real cause of most vibrations that look like a "PID problem." Make sure the hardware is clean before trying to fix it in software.
</Callout>

## Blackbox: not blindly, but with data

<KeyTerm en="blackbox">Blackbox</KeyTerm> is a "black box recorder" that logs all the data inside the drone during flight (gyro motion, motor outputs, commands) thousands of times per second. Afterwards you can review this recording on a computer and see **what happened and when.**

The intuition blackbox gives you is this: instead of trying to "feel," you **see.** For example:

- How well do the drone's requested motion (setpoint) and its actual motion (gyro) overlap?
- At what frequency is the vibration? (This tells you which filter is needed.)
- Are the motors working needlessly (constantly making corrections)?

<Callout type="info">
Reading the blackbox in fine detail is an advanced topic; the goal here is to gain **intuition**: tuning is not a guessing game but a job done by measuring and observing. You want to say "the oscillation decreased on the graph" rather than "it felt better."
</Callout>

## How PID changes affect flight feel

<KeyTerm en="PID">PID</KeyTerm> is the three settings that determine "how the drone corrects the difference between the motion I want and the actual motion." Very roughly, in terms of flight feel:

- **P (Proportional):** How **hard** the drone responds to error. Too low P = soft, loose, "spongy" feel. Too high P = sharp, but past a point **vibration/oscillation** begins.
- **I (Integral):** How **stubbornly** the drone holds a direction (e.g. keeping its nose steady in wind). Too low I = the drone "drifts," can't hold the requested angle. Too high I = a stiff, slow-to-recover feel.
- **D (Derivative):** The setting that **suppresses/dampens** oscillation; it reins in P's excess. But D heats the motors, and too much of it leads to noise (and heat).

<Callout type="tip">
Intuitive summary: P is "how hard," I is "how stubbornly it holds," D is "how much it dampens oscillation." Don't think of a single value in isolation, think of the balance: often if you increase P, you also need to look at D to rein it in.
</Callout>

## The filter vs latency balance

<KeyTerm en="filter">Filters</KeyTerm> clean the unwanted **noise/vibration** in the gyro and motor signals. Without filters this noise passes to the motors, causing heating and blurry video. It might sound like "the more filtering the better" — but it has a cost.

<KeyTerm en="latency">Latency</KeyTerm> is the time it takes for a command/sensing to turn into an effect. Every filter, while "smoothing" the signal, adds a bit of **latency** to it. Here's the fundamental balance:

- **Too heavy filtering:** Noise is cleaned well, but the drone gets "lazy," responds late to commands, the feel becomes blurry, and it can actually become more prone to oscillation.
- **Too light filtering:** The response is sharp and lively, but since the noise isn't cleaned, the motors heat up and vibration increases.

<Callout type="warning">
The goal is not "as much filtering as possible," but **the least filtering that does the job.** Unnecessarily heavy filtering can amplify the latency/responsiveness problem you're trying to solve. If your hardware is clean (well balanced, solid), you'll need less filtering anyway.
</Callout>

## Progress in small steps

The most important practical rule of tuning: **one thing at a time, in small steps.**

1. **One variable:** Don't change P, D, and the filter at the same time. Change one thing, fly/measure, see the result.
2. **Small step:** Don't make big jumps; test with a small change. A big step can amplify the problem without you understanding where it came from.
3. **Save and compare:** Note which setting gave which result (and compare with blackbox). Answer "did it get better?" with data.
4. **Be able to revert:** Back up a working setting (e.g. stock or a good ready-made tune) so you can return if things get worse.

<Callout type="info">
**Summary:** Tuning is not mandatory on every drone; the defaults are usually good. Turn to it when you see a symptom (vibration, jello, bad feel) and after first ruling out **mechanics.** Progress not blindly but by measuring with **blackbox.** P = stiffness, I = holding stubbornness, D = oscillation damping; filters clean noise but add **latency** — settle for the least filtering. Do everything **one variable at a time, in small steps**, and keep a backup so you can revert. Now a mini quiz!
</Callout>
