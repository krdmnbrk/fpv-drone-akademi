By now you know how to hold the drone steady in the air (hover). The next step is to **turn the drone's nose** without breaking the hover. The motion that does this is called <KeyTerm en="yaw">yaw</KeyTerm>.

## What is the yaw axis?

A drone has three axes of rotation. They're easy to remember by thinking of your head:

- **Pitch** — tilting your head forward/back (drone nose down/up).
- **Roll** — tilting your head onto your shoulder (drone leans right/left).
- **Yaw** — turning your head left/right as if saying "no" (drone nose turns right/left).

<KeyTerm en="yaw">Yaw</KeyTerm> rotates the drone **around a vertical axis** — that is, in place. The drone doesn't drift sideways or climb; only the direction it faces changes. Just like standing still and turning around your own axis.

<Callout type="info">
Important distinction: **roll** **moves** the drone sideways, while **yaw** **rotates** the drone in place. Both feel like "left-right," but they do completely different things. We'll use this distinction a lot in the next lesson (coordinated turns).
</Callout>

## Which stick controls yaw?

On a standard Mode 2 radio, **yaw is the left stick's left/right movement.** (The left stick's up/down is throttle.)

- Push the left stick **right** and the nose turns **right**.
- Push the left stick **left** and the nose turns **left**.
- The more you push the stick, the **faster** it turns; release it and the rotation stops.

<Callout type="warning">
This is the thing beginners mix up most often: throttle and yaw are **on the same stick**. While trying to turn the nose, it's easy to accidentally move the stick up/down and touch the throttle. So while learning yaw, pay extra attention to keeping the throttle steady.
</Callout>

## Combining yaw while holding a hover

The real skill is **not losing the hover** while rotating. When a drone turns its nose, two things get harder:

1. **Throttle must stay steady.** Since yaw is the horizontal movement of the left stick, it's easy to accidentally touch the throttle (the vertical movement). The drone shouldn't descend or climb while rotating.
2. **Your sense of orientation changes.** When the nose turns toward you, the controls **feel reversed**: with the drone facing you, "roll right" is to your left. This is the moment new pilots struggle with most.

So do the exercise in **small steps**: first turn 90 degrees, stop, recover the hover, then a bit more. Don't try to spin the full turn at once.

## A classic exercise: nose always outward

The classic way to reinforce yaw + hover: keep the drone at eye level and slowly rotate it around its own axis **with the nose always facing away from you**. While doing this:

- Hold the throttle steady to keep your altitude.
- Turn the nose with only small yaw inputs.
- If the drone drifts from its spot, bring it back with tiny roll/pitch corrections.

Once this exercise feels easy, also try rotating with the **nose facing toward you** — getting used to the reversed-control feel will help you a lot later.

<Callout type="tip">
Do all of this practice **in the simulator.** Combining yaw + hover is confusing at first, and trying it dozens of times in the simulator is free. Making the same mistake on a real drone can break a propeller or the frame.
</Callout>
