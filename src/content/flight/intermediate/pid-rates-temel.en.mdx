As you browse Betaflight, you'll see two settings called "PID" and "Rates." They sound intimidating and engineering-like — but their logic is actually very intuitive. In this lesson we'll understand **what they are**; fine-tuning is a topic for the advanced level.

## PID: How does the drone maintain balance?

A drone doesn't sit perfectly still in the air — it's constantly fighting wind, vibration, and small imbalances. The system that keeps it balanced is <KeyTerm en="PID controller">PID</KeyTerm>.

Think of PID as an **automatic correction loop.** Thousands of times per second, the FC asks: *"Is there a difference between the angle the drone should be at and its current angle?"* If so, it adjusts the motors to close that difference. This difference is called the **error**, and PID's three letters tackle this error from three different angles:

- **P (Proportional):** "How far off am I right now?" If the error is large, it makes a large correction. It's the main driving force of PID.
- **I (Integral):** "Have I been slightly off for a long time?" It accumulates small but persistent deviations over time (e.g. a wind constantly pushing sideways) and corrects them.
- **D (Derivative):** "How fast am I deviating?" It's the brake that keeps the correction from overshooting and **oscillating**.

<Callout type="info">
An everyday analogy: like staying balanced on a bicycle. **P** = "I leaned to one side, so I immediately steer the other way." **I** = "the wind keeps pushing me left, so I lean slightly right." **D** = "I steered too hard, so I soften it to avoid swerving." The drone does this thousands of times per second, for you.
</Callout>

A well-tuned PID makes the drone feel solid and "locked-in." If it's poorly tuned, the drone shakes, oscillates, or responds late to commands. But note: **the PID of off-the-shelf drones is already reasonably tuned.**

## Rates: rotation speed relative to the stick

PID is about *how* the drone balances itself. <KeyTerm en="rates">Rates</KeyTerm> are something entirely different: they determine **how fast the drone rotates for a given amount of stick.**

So rates set how **gentle or sharp** the controls feel:

- **Low rates:** Even if you push the stick all the way, the drone rotates slowly. A smooth, calm, forgiving feel — comfortable for beginners and cinematic flying.
- **High rates:** Even a small stick movement rotates the drone very fast. A sharp, aggressive feel — for fast freestyle and flips/rolls.

<Callout type="tip">
Rates aren't a "right/wrong" setting, they're a **preference** setting. Some pilots like it smooth, others like it very sharp. Both are valid; what matters is which feels comfortable in your hands.
</Callout>

## When is tuning needed?

Here's the most important and most reassuring part:

<Callout type="warning">
**For most beginners, the default settings are more than enough.** The default PID and rates values of a new drone or of Betaflight are chosen by experienced developers to "fly well." You can fly comfortably for months without touching PID/rates — and you should.
</Callout>

PID/rates are usually adjusted in the following situations (and they're all advanced-level):

- If the drone has a visible **shake/oscillation** (a PID issue).
- If the controls feel too slow or too sharp to you (a rates preference).
- If you built your own drone and want to tune it specifically.

If none of these apply, **the best "tuning" is not touching anything at all.** Develop your flying feel first by **flying a lot on the sticks**; tinkering with numbers comes last. We'll cover PID/filter fine-tuning in detail in the advanced **tuning** lesson.
