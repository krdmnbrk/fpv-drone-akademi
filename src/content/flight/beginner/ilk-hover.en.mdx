We learned throttle and the hover point. Now we come to the first real goal that underpins all FPV skills: the **hover.**

A <KeyTerm en="hover">hover</KeyTerm> means holding the drone at a single point in the air, steady and balanced — it neither climbs, descends, nor drifts in any direction. It sounds simple, but it's actually the most important skill in flying: if you can keep a drone steady, every other maneuver is built on top of it.

<Callout type="tip">
Always do your first hovers in **Angle mode.** Remember: in Angle mode the drone levels itself out when you release the stick and tries to hold position. This gives you a lot of confidence on your first hover; even if you panic and let go of the sticks, the drone tries to recover itself.
</Callout>

## A hover is really continuous small corrections

Most beginners expect the drone to "just sit there like a rock" in the air. In reality, a hover is like balancing a broom handle on your palm: the drone starts to drift slightly one way, you make a small correction, it drifts a bit the other way, you correct again. A hover is the sum of these **continuous small corrections.**

You make these corrections with two stick movements:

- <KeyTerm en="roll">Roll</KeyTerm> — leans the drone **left/right**. If the drone starts drifting right, you pull it back with a slight left roll.
- <KeyTerm en="pitch">Pitch</KeyTerm> — tilts the drone's nose **forward/back**. If the drone is creeping forward, you balance it with a slight back pitch.

<Callout type="warning">
The key word is **small.** When the drone drifts one way, don't make a big, harsh correction — that throws you in the opposite direction and starts "oscillation" (the drone swinging back and forth). Use tiny touches and correct **little by little** the moment you notice the drift. Try to keep the throttle steady at the hover point at the same time: you're smoothly managing throttle and roll/pitch all at once.
</Callout>

## Start at eye level, close, and low

For your first hover, keep the drone **at eye level** (or slightly below), close to you, and at a **low** altitude:

- **Eye level** is where you can see the drone's orientation (which way it's leaning, where the nose is pointing) most clearly.
- Being **close and low** makes control easier and minimizes crash damage if you make a mistake.

<Callout type="info">
While hovering very close to the ground you may notice an effect: the air pushed down by the propellers reflects back up off the surface and forms a kind of **"air cushion"** under the drone — this is called <KeyTerm en="ground effect">ground effect</KeyTerm>. Ground effect is actually a **lifting and stabilizing** effect: it takes less throttle to stay at the same height and the drone feels a bit more "settled." The actual restlessness you feel very close to the ground comes not from this cushion but from the propeller air hitting the surface and flowing **turbulently/irregularly** (especially over rough or dusty ground). In other words: ground effect lifts you; what creates the restlessness is the irregular airflow at that height. Even so, staying low at the start is far safer than falling from a height; first learn to hold steady low, then increase the altitude little by little.
</Callout>

## The disarm reflex without panicking

This is perhaps the most life-saving part of this lesson. When a hover attempt goes wrong — the drone suddenly darts off in one direction, spins, gets out of control — a beginner's instinct is to panic and jerk the sticks around at random. This almost always makes things worse.

The correct reflex is one thing: **disarm.** Flip your arming switch off. Remember, disarming stops the motors instantly. Instead of trying to save an out-of-control drone with the sticks, cutting the motors and letting it fall where it is is often much safer.

<Callout type="tip">
On your first flights, burn this into your mind: **"If something goes wrong — don't think — disarm."** Practice this reflex deliberately in the simulator: intentionally lose control of the drone and try to disarm immediately. In real flight this reflex protects both your drone and the people around you. Disarming from low = minor damage; panicking and shooting up high = major damage.
</Callout>

## What's next?

Nailing the hover takes some repetition — be patient and practice a lot in the simulator. When you can do it, you've started thinking like a real pilot. The next and final beginner lesson will summarize the **safety rules and Türkiye's SHGM regulations** you need to know before going outside.
