In the previous lesson we learned that FPV means "looking through the drone's eyes." Now let's compare it with the **other** way of flying a drone: **flying by eye.**

## Two different ways of "seeing"

**Flying by eye** — in English this is called <KeyTerm en="line-of-sight, LOS">line-of-sight (LOS)</KeyTerm>. Here the pilot stands on the ground, looks up, and flies by **looking directly at the drone with their own eyes**. Toy helicopters and most entry-level drones are flown this way.

**FPV flying** — the pilot looks not at the sky but at the **camera image in the goggles**. Even when the drone is far away or behind an obstacle, the pilot keeps seeing it "from within."

## The critical difference: FPV is NOT Visual Line of Sight (VLOS)

There's a very important concept in aviation: <KeyTerm en="Visual Line of Sight, VLOS">VLOS (Visual Line of Sight)</KeyTerm>. This means the pilot stays in direct visual contact with the aircraft **with the naked eye, without any aid**.

When you fly with FPV goggles, you look at the aircraft not with your naked eye but **through a camera and goggles**. Therefore:

<Callout type="warning">
**FPV flying, by definition, does not count as Visual Line of Sight (VLOS).** Because you see the drone through a camera rather than with your own eyes. This is a very important distinction, both for safety and legally.
</Callout>

Why does it matter? Because when you put on goggles, your field of view narrows: you only focus on the direction the camera is pointing. You **can't see** a bird passing by, a person approaching you, a tree off to the side, or another aircraft. To close this gap, you need one more person.

## The solution: a spotter (visual observer)

A <KeyTerm en="spotter / visual observer">spotter</KeyTerm> is the person who **continuously watches the drone with the naked eye** while you fly with goggles. Their duties:

- Track the drone visually and know where it is.
- Watch for hazards around it (approaching people, vehicles, other aircraft, obstacles) and **warn you out loud**.
- Alert you immediately when something goes wrong (e.g. the drone heading in a dangerous direction).

<Callout type="tip">
A practical rule: **don't put on goggles and fly alone.** Have a spotter beside you tracking the drone by eye. This is both the safest habit and — as we'll note in the next lesson — part of flying in compliance with the rules in Türkiye.
</Callout>

## How is this handled in Türkiye?

In Türkiye, drones are called **İHA** (Unmanned Aerial Vehicle) and the rules are set by **SHGM** (the Directorate General of Civil Aviation). Hobby/sport flights are expected to be conducted **within the pilot's field of view**. Since FPV does not count as Visual Line of Sight (VLOS), the way to effectively satisfy the field-of-view requirement — and the **safest and most regulation-compliant** way — is to have a **visual observer (spotter)** watching the drone with the naked eye.
