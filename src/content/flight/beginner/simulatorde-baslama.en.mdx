In the previous lesson we saw that Acro mode is essential for real FPV, but that the drone won't save you. So how do we learn this control without crashing and breaking parts? The answer is one word: **simulator.**

A <KeyTerm en="simulator">simulator</KeyTerm> is a game that faithfully imitates a real FPV flight on your computer. You connect your own radio to the computer and fly on screen as if it were your real drone. If you crash, all you do is press a button to launch the drone again from scratch.

## Why a simulator first? (the most important part of this lesson)

A beginner pilot is almost certain to crash on their first flights. The difference is: where will you have those crashes?

- **Cheap.** Every time you break a real drone you replace a prop, a motor, even the frame; that's both money and time. Crashing in a simulator is free.
- **Safe.** In a simulator you can't harm anyone or anything. You learn without all that "what if I hit something?" stress.
- **Unlimited practice.** No worries about weather, batteries, location, or permission. At midnight you can attempt 200 takeoffs back to back. Muscle memory is built precisely through this kind of repetition.

<Callout type="tip">
Nearly all professional racing and freestyle pilots still practice in simulators. A simulator isn't "a beginner's toy"; it's **a serious pilot's training tool.** Spending your first weeks here makes you far more prepared when you move to a real drone.
</Callout>

## Popular FPV simulators

They all do the same basic job — connect your radio and teach you to fly in Acro mode. The difference between them is mostly graphics, maps, and price:

| Simulator | What stands out |
|---|---|
| **Liftoff** | Very popular, realistic freestyle and racing; lots of customization. A beginner favorite. |
| **Velocidrone** | The choice of racing pilots; accurate physics and online race tracks. |
| **Uncrashed** | Modern graphics, a smooth and comfortable starting experience. |
| **DRL (Drone Racing League) Sim** | The official racing league simulator; often on sale, event-focused. |

<Callout type="info">
Don't get hung up on which one to choose. Starting with any of them is **far** better than not starting at all. If you're on a tight budget, grab whichever one is on sale; the stick control you learn carries over to all simulators and to a real drone alike.
</Callout>

## Set your radio to Mode 2 (why is it the standard?)

Before you start, check your radio's stick layout: the de facto standard in FPV is <KeyTerm en="Mode 2">Mode 2</KeyTerm>, and everything we teach is based on it. In Mode 2 the sticks are arranged like this:

- **Left stick:** throttle (up/down) and yaw (turn left/right).
- **Right stick:** pitch (forward/back) and roll (lean left/right).

There's a practical reason almost everyone uses Mode 2 as the standard: throttle lands on the left stick, which **doesn't self-center** (it isn't spring-loaded), so the throttle level you set stays put; while pitch/roll, which steer the flight, are grouped on the **spring-centered** right stick. This separation makes it easier to steer while keeping throttle steady. Since virtually all tutorial videos, simulator defaults, and the community talk in terms of Mode 2, **choose Mode 2 at the start** and stick with it.

<Callout type="tip">
When you first set up your radio, make sure it's on Mode 2 (most radios ship from the factory in Mode 2). If you get used to a different mode, it's hard to switch later; learning from the very beginning with the community standard makes it easier both to follow the lessons and to get help from others.
</Callout>

## Connecting the radio to a computer over USB

In a simulator you should fly with your **real FPV radio**, not a mouse or a gamepad. The goal is to learn the stick feel of that radio. Most modern radios connect to a computer very easily:

1. Plug your radio into the computer with a **USB cable**.
2. From the radio's startup menu, select **"USB Joystick"** (or a similar "Game Controller") mode. This mode presents the radio's sticks to the computer like a game controller.
3. Go to the simulator's settings (Controls / Calibration) section and **calibrate the sticks**: the program will ask you to move each stick to its extremes in turn.

<KeyTerm en="USB Joystick mode">USB Joystick mode</KeyTerm> is the most practical way to use your radio directly as a game controller, without any extra receiver or hardware.

<Callout type="warning">
Don't skip calibration. If the sticks aren't calibrated correctly, the drone keeps drifting even when you've released the stick, and this causes you to learn a bad habit from the start. Calibrate it correctly once, then fly with peace of mind.
</Callout>
